Personal Statement


My name is Teng and I am currently a bioinformatics data scientist at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center in New York city. I am co-leading a research study on clonal hematopoiesis, or DNA damages in the blood that might lead to secondary leukemia through analyzing the genetic data of 35,000 patients, and am a part of a precision medicine board that aims to utilize modern genomic sequencing technologies to identify treatment options for children who have advanced stages of cancer. In ten years, I aspire to be a research leader addressing the problem of cancer by understanding the disease using modern computation and genomics. I would like to share with you my journey in the past few years, about how I found the right problem for me to solve, with the right tool for me to use.

Raised by two parents that immigrated from the rural regions of China to the capital city Beijing on their own, I inherited their quality of self-reliance and perseverance. Whenever difficulty comes my way, my mother, instead of offering help straight away, always told me that “if there is a problem, then there is a solution”, and encouraged me to resolve it on my own. My father inspired my early interest in biotechnology. An inventive engineer, he passed on to me the early excitement of the booming biotechnology industry that he experienced during the 1990s through his stories about fuel-producing bacteria and living biomaterials. My parents have always encouraged me to be on the front edge of science and technology, solving a problem that people care about.

Beginning college at Washington University was my first opportunity to see the forefront of science. I initially started my study with biology because of its profound impact on human health. I reached out to research labs and got involved in a variety of projects: I helped out at an experimental cell biology lab that studies the microenvironment of tumors, and worked a summer at Wake Forest Institute for Regenerative Medicine to do an independent research project on a technology called 3D bioprinting that has the potential to engineer whole organs. I soon discovered that because of my inclination to think quantitatively and precisely, I was always the most excited about the data analysis part of biomedical research. This led me to discover an emerging field at the intersection of biology and quantitative sciences: computational genomics, where the large volume of biological data generated by modern high-throughput sequencing technologies allows me to put my quantitative thinking into use. Hoping to get a solid foundation in both disciplines, I took on two majors in computer science and biology entering junior year.

At the same time, I found my way to a cancer genomics lab at the Mcdonnell Genome Institute. A formidable, iconic disease affecting one in three of us during our lifetime, cancer struck me as the biggest unsolved problem in human health. I was excited to learn that computational genomics offers an unprecedented opportunity to systematically understand cancer and discover new treatments. I marveled at the super-computing clusters in the lab that are able to crunch terabytes of genomic data everyday, and was fascinated by the computational algorithms that have the power to decipher on the molecular level what went wrong in cancer. After a year of research, I made my first contribution to cancer genomics via a second author paper.

Eager to see how bioinformatics is applied in the industry, I started an internship with Roche Pharmaceuticals in the San Francisco Bay. Outside of work, the mixture of biotechnology companies and the tech startup culture also provided me a unique opportunity to explore my new-found role of a computational biologist in the real world. A weekend evening found me at a Google office building among two hundred excited biologists and computer scientists who gathered for a unique purpose: to hack a man’s genomic data. Speaking in front of the crowd was serial technology entrepreneur Onno Faber, who was recently diagnosed with NF2, a rare genetic condition causing tumor growth in the nervous system for which no cure is available. However, Onno was not someone to sit passively after receiving the bad news. “I’ve always been a problem solver. These problems could cause a lot of amazing solutions. And my tumor is one of them”. Hearing from his scientist friend that genome sequencing could offer immediate insights to individual patients, he sequenced his own genome and offered his DNA data at the hackathon. Hundreds of professionals and researchers came, traveling from universities, tech companies, and pharmaceuticals. Surrounded by this diverse crowd, I could feel enthusiasm palpable in the air.

With backgrounds in both computer science and biology, I found myself in a unique position to assemble a team. Expertise in both sides were essential: without understanding the biology, it was hard to make something that will actually help the patient; without the technical know-how, it was hard to make anything at all. I therefore first contacted a post-doctoral researcher at UCSF who studied NF2 in her research, and then two computer scientists to join the team. Since most of the computer scientists had no understanding of genetics, and a lot of the biologists there had never coded before, communication became the key throughout the whole hackathon. I facilitated the discussion from explaining the central dogma to the computer scientists to introducing the concept of deep learning to biologists.

To accomplish something meaningful in forty-eight hours was not easy. Before we could apply any fancy machine learning techniques, we needed to process a hundred gigabytes of raw RNA-Seq data into feature vectors usable by the algorithm. Since I was already doing similar work at my internship, I took up that responsibility. However, with limited computational resources provided by the hackathon, each processing step would take a long time. Starting from the morning for eight hours straight, I struggled with Google's cloud computing server and new bioinformatics tools that I have never used before, trying to get the results to my team in time. Night fell and I hit yet another obstacle in processing the gene expression data. Part of the data was missing, and it was hard to recover the information. The piece of information was processing crucial for the analysis, and without it we couldn't make any helpful claims about the disease. I was burning with eagerness to get the data to my teammates, but at the same time was hitting my physical and mental limit. Knowing that my teammates were also working hard on their respective tasks, I tried to ask for their support without distracting them from their own work. The night went on, and time was running out before the final presentation. We decided that in the worst case, we would go without my part of the data, and call it a night. Just when everyone was standing up from the table and putting on jackets, I stood up yelling "YES!!" that my work-around solution finally worked. Came Sunday, our presentation impressed the judges, placing us first in the hackathon.

Leaving the event with lingering excitement, I found my direction suddenly clear. The success of Onno’s hackathon made me realize that bioinformatics is not only a powerful tool in biomedical research, but also a never-before-seen bridge that can bring science in papers published yesterday to the human endpoint in a matter of hours. Coming back to school, I further built up my knowledge base with advanced courses in data science and computational biology. I was ready for my next goal: to apply bioinformatics in a real clinic setting.

Upon graduation, I interviewed at three major genome sequencing centers in the US. At the first two centers I was told that the application of bioinformatics in the clinic was still a few years away. When I interviewed at Elli’s lab at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center, I was surprised to find that the hospital was already applying a clinical sequencing assay, MSK-IMPACT, to profile the genomes of every patient at MSK and using those information to guide treatment decisions. Most importantly, she offered me the opportunity to practice precision oncology first-hand: I would analyze real patient data and present at clinical decision meetings weekly. As soon as I left the interview, I was convinced that MSK was the next stop for me.

Every tumor is caused by a unique set of genetic abnormalities. Deciphering a cancer genome not only informs us about the cause of the disease but also guides us as to what we can do to intervene. Walking into my first precision oncology board meeting at MSK with a laptop, I sat down with a roomful of oncologists, geneticists, and clinical researchers. My team then started presenting our analysis of the DNA and RNA data of individual patients and discussing them case-by-case. I was overwhelmed by the fast exchange full of jargons in multiple domains of biology and medicine. Realizing the gaps in my knowledge, I crammed in as many papers as I could everyday.

One day, a special genome puzzled everyone: for an advanced-stage cancer, this patient’s genome was surprisingly silent. We could not find any oncogenic mutation that could explain the disease after examining all 2.2 billion base pairs of her genome. If this was the case, we would not be able to do anything for the patient besides standard radiation therapies. “What about the mutation on NFIB?” I broke the silence. It was the only gene-disrupting variant in the report, but did not have any clear biological effect. “NFIB is on chromosome 9. There is a translocation near there,” a senior scientist in the room observed, “the other end links to where MYB is.” MYB is an oncogene frequently mutated in cancers, whose hyper-activity is known to cause cancer. It naturally came to us to the possibility that a gene fusion (two genes brought together through a structural change in the genome) could have caused such a phenomenon. Popping open our computers, we wrote a few lines of code for a quick analysis of the patient’s RNA expression data, which measure the activity of the genes in the tumor. We indeed found signs of over-expression of MYB. The meeting ended on a good note.

Leaving the meeting, I was not satisfied by the limited evidence that we had gathered. Although we had proven that oncogene MYB was hyper-active, it was still unexplained what caused this dysregulation in the first place. Furthermore, there was no real evidence of a gene fusion event in the data. It occured to me that the problem might lay beyond the genomic level - enhancer hijacking. I immediately dived into the literature, looking for past research and patient cases from other cancer centers around the world that might shed light on ours. Then I found it: a patient treated at Harvard Cancer Center just two years ago had the exact same structural change as ours, and the research team found a huge clusters of super-enchancers near NFIB, confirming our hypothesis. What’s more, the authors conducted a pre-clinical trial that showed that by applying bromodomain inhibitor, a targeted drug that is able to suppress enhancer activities, they could induce the tumor to shrink significantly. Exalted, I shared the findings with the team. We subsequently presented to the oncologists, who were impressed with our answers and agreed with our interpretation. My team celebrated the triumph.

A few weeks passed while I was expecting to hear further news about the patient, until I ran into her oncologist one day at the hospital. When I asked if they had decided to give the targeted therapy to the patient, the oncologist said that although he found our suggestions scientifically valid, he eventually could not act on what we found. I was perplexed. He further explained that because these research findings were still too novel, making clinical decisions based on them would be too risky. The reality was that they simply did not know enough to decide whether our new piece of information would harm or benefit the patient.

Leaving work that day, I reflected on the reason why we could not have done more to help this patient even though we had the most up-to-date set of tools and knowledge base. It is true that fast sequencing technologies coupled with bioinformatic analysis can deliver potentially useful information for an individual. However, our biggest limitation lies in our ability to interpret this information. We would not have been able to interpret Onno’s tumor genome if we did not have other genomic profiles with the same disease to compare. Similarly, we could not have found the cause for the MSK patient’s cancer if a thorough study had not been conducted. To improve our ability to interpret a patient’s genomic information and eventually to intervene using personalized cancer treatments, we need to collect more genomic data, enroll more patients in clinical trials, and conduct more systematic studies with the aim of understanding the disease on a fundamental level. The desire to play a role in leading this important next step of precision oncology brings me to where I am today.

I am excited for the revolutionary changes that next generation sequencing technologies are bringing to our understanding and treatment of cancer, and feel grateful for finding my role in this bigger picture as a bioinformatics scientist, at the intersection of what I love to do and what I care about. Harnessing the power of genomic big data and bringing it into the clinic requires interdisciplinary expertise in biomedicine, statistics, as well as computer science. Being a leader in this effort demands not only scientific training but also the ability to engage the scientific community, the industry, and the general public. I will be delighted to pursue such skill set through a PhD study.
